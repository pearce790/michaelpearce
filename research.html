Recent Projects

Unified modeling of score and ranking data applied to grant panel review
Rankings and scores are two common data types used by judges to express preferences and/or perceptions of quality in a collection of objects. Numerous models exist to study data of each type separately, but no unified statistical model captures both data types simultaneously without first performing data conversion. We propose the Mallows-Binomial model to close this gap, which combines a Mallows ranking model with Binomial score models through shared parameters that quantify object quality, a consensus ranking, and the level of consensus among judges. We propose an efficient tree-search algorithm to calculate the exact MLE of model parameters, study statistical properties of the model both analytically and through simulation, and apply our model to real data from an instance of grant panel review that collected both scores and partial rankings. Furthermore, we demonstrate how model outputs can be used to rank objects with confidence. The proposed model is shown to sensibly combine information from both scores and rankings to quantify object quality and measure consensus with appropriate levels of statistical uncertainty. The work was published in July 2022 in the Journal of Machine Learning Research, with additional theoretical results regarding bootstrap validity published on arXiv.

Probabilistic forecasting of maximum human lifespan to 2100 using Bayesian population projections
In this project with Professor Adrian E. Raftery, we quantified the human lifespan using a statistical approach that probabilistically forecasts the maximum reported age at death (MRAD) through 2100. Using the existing exponential survival model for supercentenarians (people over age 110), we quantify population uncertainty using Bayesian population projections and incorporate the most recent data from the International Database on Longevity to obtain unconditional estimates of the distribution of MRAD this century in a fully Bayesian analysis. This work was published in June 2021 in the journal Demographic Research and has since been featured in a number of major news outlets such as the Washington Post, BBC News, and CNBC.

stoBEST: An efficient methodology for increased spatial resolution in two-component molecular tagging velocimetry
This NSF-funded project was completed as a senior undergraduate student at St. Olaf College as part of the Center for Interdisciplinary Research. We developed a new methodology to read and analyze pairs of molecular tagging velocimetry images, a technique for studying high-speed gaseous flows in a chemical experiment. The proposed method de-noises and processes the original images and then uses a variety of nonparametric techniques to create high-resolution two-component velocity fields. Our results are compared against numerous existing alternatives and shown to improve accuracy and resolution in a variety of cases. This work was published in 2020 in the journal Measurement Science and Technology.
